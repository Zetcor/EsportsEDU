The International

The International: The Pinnacle of Dota 2 Esports

The International was inaugurated in 2011 by Valve Corporation as the inaugural tournament for Dota 2, held during Gamescom in Cologne, Germany. It instantly garnered global attention due to its then-unprecedented $1.6 million prize pool, fully funded by Valve. Over the years, The International has become a benchmark in esports, with its prize pool consistently breaking industry records—peaking at over $40 million in 2021. The introduction of the Battle Pass system in 2013 allowed fans to contribute to the prize pool, revolutionizing funding models in esports. TI has evolved significantly, moving across different host cities including Seattle, Vancouver, Shanghai, Bucharest, and Singapore. The competition has maintained its prestige and competitive rigor, drawing the world’s best Dota 2 teams and serving as the conclusion of the annual Dota Pro Circuit (DPC) season.

Organizer/Governing Body

Valve Corporation, the developer and publisher of Dota 2, serves as the exclusive organizer and governing body of The International.

Games Featured in the Tournament

Only one title is featured: Dota 2, a multiplayer online battle arena (MOBA) game developed by Valve, known for its strategic complexity and steep learning curve.

Tournament Format

The International is one of the most prestigious tournaments in esports, featuring 18 of the top Dota 2 teams from around the world. Twelve teams qualify by earning points throughout the Dota Pro Circuit (DPC) season, while six more secure their spots through regional qualifiers across North America, South America, Europe, China, and Southeast Asia. Once at the tournament, teams are split into two groups for a round-robin Group Stage. The top four teams in each group move to the upper playoff bracket, while those finishing fifth to eighth drop into the lower bracket.

The Main Event follows with a double-elimination playoff bracket, where teams must lose twice to be eliminated. Most matches are best-of-three, except for the Grand Final, which is a best-of-five series. Held in a major global city, the event is played entirely on-site to ensure fairness and create a thrilling experience for fans. The International is a celebration of elite competition and the global Dota 2 community.

Prize Pool

The International’s prize pool is funded through a mix of Valve’s base contribution and community support via in-game Battle Pass purchases, resulting in some of the largest payouts in esports. TI10 in 2021 set the record with over $40 million, while other editions like TI9 and TI11 offered $34 million and $18 million, respectively. The first-place team typically takes around 45% of the total, with second place receiving about 13–15%, and the rest distributed across lower placements. This structure not only rewards top competitors but also ensures meaningful earnings for all participants, reinforcing community involvement in the event’s success.

Live Streams and Coverage

The International offers extensive global coverage, ensuring fans around the world can follow the action live. Matches are broadcast across multiple platforms and in numerous languages, making the tournament accessible to a diverse and international audience. Venue and Production

The production value of The International is renowned for its cinematic quality. It features elaborate stage designs, stunning LED displays, and captivating team walk-ins that create a truly immersive viewing experience. The emotional storytelling in the broadcasts, highlighting team rivalries and player journeys, adds an entertainment dimension that goes beyond the gameplay, making it a spectacle that appeals to both esports fans and general audiences alike.

Global Stage

The International has been held in various cities around the world, each chosen for its cultural significance and esports infrastructure. It started in 2011 in Cologne, Germany, and has since taken place in several major cities, including Seattle, USA (2012–2017), Vancouver, Canada (2018), Shanghai, China (2019), Bucharest, Romania (2021), Singapore (2022), and Seattle again in 2023, marking a return to the United States. Each venue has hosted the event with a unique atmosphere, helping to build the event's prestige and global recognition.
